Thinking about Buying an Apartment

 

 

  I am thinking about getting an apartment in Brisbane - here is some research and photos of the area.

  

  

 

How do you discern a quality apartment from one frought with problems??

Here is a checklist of potential pitfalls and pluses.

(Source: 'The Domain' Sydney Morning Herald, July 19, 2001)

Construction

Acoustics are a major issue in new apartments, To check the quality of the acoustics, try arming yourself with a ghetto blaster in a neighboring apartment.  Or, sit quietly in the early evening and if you can hear your neighbours, worry.  If buying off the plan, you should ask the developer for a guarantee of the target acoustic rating "as built".  Then employ an acoustic engineer to test it.

General construction problems: look for cracks, damp or signs of movement in the building.  Foor and walls should appear plumb.  Look, too, for any variations in the rendering.  Just about anything outside that is higher than inside, like a planter or balcony is a potential water entry from above.  Garden beds on balconies and roofs need careful waterproofing.  

You should get a building inspection.  With off-the-plan apartments, go in and do a defects list before you settle.  Providing you're fair and reasonable, most builders will be happy to fix problems.  

The #1 concern is the developer.  Do they have a track record?  Visiting other properties by the same developer/builder team is a canny move; it displays a physical track record and how the work waers with time.  Make sure the builders have insurance, so if there is a problem, it can be fixed.  For an off-the-plan apartment, the Dept of Fair Trading says the developer should attach a certificate showing home warranty insurance at settlement.

Plumbing and Electricity

Here are a few quick tests to identify potential problem areas: turn all taps on and off quickly; if there is a thunking sound the plumbing joints will probably crack or lossen within 3 to 5 years.  Check water pressure and temperature by turning on several taps at once.  Flush toilets then check to see if they have shut off properly and don't leak.

There can also be air-conditioning malfunctions.  Get the acoustics on air conditioners check professionally.  Electrical problems are less common, but you can test all fittings with a powerpoint tester, available at most hardware stores.  It will pick up hitches, such as a point that is not properly earthed.

Fittings and Materials

Fittings and materials should be of a reasonable quality, though this will vary according to budget and the developer.  Don't assume quality just because you have paid a high price.  Most complaints to the Dept of Fair Trading concern the quality of finishes such as the bathroom and kitchen fittings.  Consumer often have unrealistic expectations "heightened by the glossy promotional material" and sales agents "spiel".  Most off-the-plan purchases include a clause allowing the builder/developer a 5% variation of floorplans or materials.  This provides a fair amount of leeway, so enlist a lawyer experienced in off-the-plan sales and have inclusions and fittings detailed into a contract, the Dept Fair Trading advises.  

If the apartment is already finished, check windows and doors: a quality sliding door will move easily and the seal is good if it cuts out noise and draft.  The same applies to windows.  Swing a door; it should feel solid, heavy, not light.  Open cupboards and check the quality of the joinery.  Get a feel - has someone taken a bit of care?  What will it look like in 10-15 years?

Floorplan

Size is important, but it is the use of space that is more important.  A ceiling height of about 3 metres, a well-resolved plan with no dead-spots, rooms that work for their intended purpose, a good flow between spaces and a link between inside and outside all contribute to a sense of comfort and space.  A balcony or courtyard is also a significant attribute, but check that it is functional.

There is also the practical aspect of judging space: will your furniture fit?  Display units use deceptive furnishings, like 3/4 beds, in order to look bigger.  Get a floorplan and see if your furniture and appliances will fit.  Also, visualise going through your daily routine.  One recent buyer of a new inner city abode was amazed to find there was no spot for the microwave and another looking for the cutlery drawer in his flash new pad realised the kitchen didn't actually have any drawers.

A small entry hall or space is also an asset.  It provides privacy when someone comes to the door.  Beware a floorplan that puts a bathroom opposite the dining room or kitchen.  In a perfect world, you would have 2 bathrooms, one for guests.  Check that the bedrooms don't back onto lift shafts (it does happen).  Find out the layout of the neighboring unit.  Ideally, rooms with the same functions back onto each other.

There should be plenty of storage.  If there are no built-ins, be realistic about the space cupboards will take up, physically and visually.  Storage in a lock-up space is perfect for items not used every day.
